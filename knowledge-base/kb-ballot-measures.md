# Wisconsin 2026 Ballot Measures

All three constitutional amendments are certified for the November 3, 2026 ballot. These were placed on the ballot by the Wisconsin State Legislature.

---

## Amendment 1: Prohibit Government Discrimination or Preferential Treatment Amendment

**Common name:** Anti-DEI Amendment

**Official ballot question:** "Governmental entity discrimination prohibited. Shall section 27 of article I of the constitution be created to prohibit governmental entities in the state from discriminating against, or granting preferential treatment to, any individual or group on the basis of race, sex, color, ethnicity, or national origin in public employment, public education, public contracting, or public administration?"

**Legislative history:** Assembly passed 54-45 (January 13, 2026). Senate passed 18-15. Assembly Joint Resolution 102 (AJR 102).

**What it does:**
- Bans state and local government from using race, sex, color, ethnicity, or national origin as criteria in:
  - Public employment (government hiring and promotion)
  - Public education (university admissions, K-12 programs)
  - Public contracting (who gets government contracts)
  - Public administration (how government programs are run)
- Would effectively end all government diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs
- Similar to amendments passed in California (Prop 209, 1996), Michigan (2006), and other states

**Context:**
- Placed on ballot by the Republican-controlled legislature on January 21, 2026
- Governor Evers opposes but cannot veto constitutional amendments
- Requires a simple majority of voters to pass
- Opponents argue it would gut minority scholarship programs, veteran hiring preferences, and programs addressing racial disparities
- Supporters argue it ensures equal treatment under the law

**Severity:** High — would fundamentally change how Wisconsin government operates programs related to race and gender.

## Amendment 2: Prohibit Government Closure of Places of Worship During Emergencies Amendment

**Common name:** Worship Closure Amendment / COVID Backlash Amendment

**Official ballot question:** "Shall Section 18 of Article I of the Constitution, which deals with religious liberty, be amended to prohibit the state or a political subdivision of the state from ordering the closure of, or forbidding gatherings in, places of worship in response to a state of emergency, including a public health emergency?"

**Legislative history:** Introduced as Assembly Joint Resolution 10 (AJR 10). Sponsored by State Rep. Ron Tusler (R-3). Legislature advanced it on January 21, 2026.

**What it does:**
- Prohibits state or local government from ordering places of worship to close during ANY declared emergency
- Includes public health emergencies (directly responding to COVID-19 church closures)
- Applies to ALL places of worship — churches, mosques, synagogues, temples, etc.
- Would override any future governor's emergency powers regarding worship gatherings

**Context:**
- Direct response to COVID-19 pandemic restrictions that closed churches in 2020
- Placed on ballot by the legislature on January 21, 2026
- Similar measures have passed in other states post-COVID
- Supporters frame it as religious liberty protection
- Opponents argue it could endanger public health during future pandemics
- Some legal scholars note tension with Establishment Clause (singling out religious institutions for special treatment)

**Severity:** Medium — narrow scope (only worship spaces during emergencies) but sets constitutional precedent.

## Amendment 3: Prohibit Partial Veto to Increase Tax or Fee Amendment

**Common name:** Partial Veto / Evers Veto Amendment

**Official ballot question:** "Partial veto. Shall section 10 (1) (c) of article V of the constitution be amended to prohibit the governor, in exercising his or her partial veto authority, from creating or increasing or authorizing the creation or increase of any tax or fee?"

**Legislative history:** Passed by legislature on February 19, 2026. Senate Joint Resolution 116 (SJR 116).

**What it does:**
- Restricts the governor's partial veto power (a power unique to Wisconsin — the governor can strike individual words, digits, or sentences from bills)
- Specifically: the governor could no longer use partial vetoes to CREATE or INCREASE any tax or fee
- Responding to Governor Evers' famous 2023 veto that extended a school funding increase for 400 years (by striking digits from "2024-25" to read "2425")

**Context:**
- Wisconsin's governor has the most powerful partial veto in the nation
- Governor Evers' 400-year veto was legal but sparked major controversy
- The legislature passed this on February 19, 2026
- Certified for the November ballot as of February 2026
- Supporters argue it prevents abuse of veto power
- Opponents argue partial veto is an important check on the legislature

**Severity:** Medium — changes the balance of power between governor and legislature.

## Local Referenda — April 7, 2026

In addition to the state constitutional amendments (November), there are 72 school district referenda on the April 7, 2026 spring ballot.

- **Total sought:** Approximately $1 billion across all 72 districts
- **Types:** Mix of operational (recurring funding) and capital construction (one-time borrowing)
- **Notable examples:**
  - Appleton Area School District: $60 million operational referendum
  - Necedah Area School District: $5.8 million operational referendum
  - Howard-Suamico and Sauk Prairie: Both operational AND capital referenda
- **Context:** More than 20% of these districts are returning to voters after failed referendums in 2025
- **Why so many:** Wisconsin's school funding formula has not kept pace with costs; districts increasingly rely on referenda to close budget gaps

To find specific referenda on your local ballot, visit [MyVote.WI.gov](https://myvote.wi.gov).
